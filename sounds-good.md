## 1. Purpose and Scope

This document explains why outputs from generative artificial intelligence systems, including large language models, are accurately characterized as "sounding good" rather than being right or factually correct. It then extends the analysis to augmented configurations, including retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), tool use and agentic systems, and sets out the verification discipline that follows for professional work. The document addresses the behavior of the underlying technology as a class. It does not assess any specific vendor product, and it does not constitute legal advice on any particular matter.

## 2. Executive Summary

- Language models are trained to produce statistically plausible text, not to produce true statements. Correctness is a frequent byproduct of that objective, not a guaranteed property of the output.
- The model has no internal mechanism distinguishing fact from fabrication. A genuine authority and an invented one are generated by the same process, in the same confident register.
- Augmentations such as RAG, tool use and agentic orchestration relocate the reliability problem to different points in the workflow. They do not eliminate it.
- Fluency is therefore not evidence of correctness. Verification obligations remain with the human professional at every point where generated content enters the work product.
- Organizations should map each AI workflow to identify its verified segments and its generated segments, and direct human review at the generated segments before any output is relied upon.

## 3. The Mechanism: Why "Sounds Good" Is Technically Accurate

A large language model is trained to predict the next unit of text given the preceding text, with the objective of minimizing prediction error against a vast corpus of human writing. The optimization target is statistical plausibility: what a competent writer would likely say next. Nothing in this training objective references truth, external reality or verifiability. Later refinement stages, such as instruction tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback, steer the model toward outputs that human raters judge to be helpful and accurate. But raters largely judge by plausibility, so the reward signal still passes through the question "does this read as a good answer" rather than "is this true."

The result is a system whose native competence is producing text that satisfies the surface criteria of a correct answer: appropriate register, confident structure, domain vocabulary and citation-shaped references. Where the underlying claim is well represented in training data, plausible and true coincide, often at very high rates. Where the claim is sparse, contested or novel, the model produces the same fluent form around unreliable content. Fabricated output, commonly called hallucination, is therefore not a malfunction. It is the ordinary operation of the mechanism in a region where plausibility and truth diverge.

Three properties make "sounds good" the precise characterization rather than a rhetorical one. First, the model holds no epistemic state distinguishing knowledge from confabulation; internally, a correct citation and an invented one are the same kind of output. Second, fluency is uniform while accuracy is variable, so the signals humans ordinarily use to assess credibility, such as articulateness, confidence and coherence, carry no information about correctness. Third, the failure mode is asymmetric: errors arrive in the most persuasive available packaging, which is more dangerous for professional reliance than errors that look like errors.

The consequences are documented. In *Mata v. Avianca, Inc.*, a federal court sanctioned attorneys who submitted a brief containing judicial opinions fabricated by a generative AI tool, complete with invented quotations and citations attributed to real judges. When one attorney asked the tool whether the cases were real, it confirmed that they existed and were available in reputable legal databases. The fabrications were fluent, formatted correctly and confidently affirmed. They sounded good. They were not right.

## 4. Augmented Configurations: Relocation, Not Resolution

Vendors increasingly market augmented systems as answers to the reliability problem. Each augmentation supplies verified material or verified computation at one point in the workflow. Everything surrounding that point, including query formulation, source selection, synthesis, interpretation and the system's own judgment that a task is complete, remains generated by the plausibility mechanism. The governing principle for professional users is that augmentations relocate the "sounds good" problem to new positions in the workflow. Knowing where it relocates is the substance of competent oversight.

### 4.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation

RAG retrieves documents relevant to a query and instructs the model to answer from them. This genuinely improves grounding, because the model paraphrases checkable text instead of reconstructing claims from training-data statistics, and it substantially reduces the classic fabrication failure of invented cases and invented statutes.

It does not make outputs true. The residual failure modes are characteristic. The retrieval step can surface the wrong documents, outdated versions or superficially relevant but substantively inapposite material, and the model will answer fluently from whatever it received. The synthesis step can misread the retrieved text, over-generalize a narrow holding, merge incompatible sources into a single confident statement or attribute to a document a claim it does not make. Citations in RAG output point to real sources, which paradoxically increases misplaced trust: the reader sees a real authority and assumes the proposition is supported, when the model may have drawn from it something it does not say. Empirical study bears this out. A 2024 Stanford evaluation of leading RAG-based legal research tools found that they still produced incorrect or unsupported answers in a significant share of benchmark queries, notwithstanding retrieval grounding. The plausibility test has moved from "does this claim sound right" to "does this reading of the source sound right," and the model applies the same standard to both.

### 4.2 Tool Use

When a model calls a calculator, a code interpreter, a database query or a search interface, the tool's output is deterministic or externally sourced rather than generated. For that segment of the chain, correctness is a property of the tool, and this is a real improvement: arithmetic performed by code execution is right in a way arithmetic performed by text prediction is not.

The generative mechanism still controls three points. First, whether to call the tool at all: a model that answers a computational question from memory instead of invoking the tool has silently substituted plausibility for computation, and the output looks identical to the user. Second, what inputs to pass: a correct database engine returns a precisely wrong answer to a plausibly wrong query. Third, interpretation: the model narrates what the tool result means, and that narration is generation. A recurring professional failure is a correct data retrieval followed by a fluent, confident and wrong characterization of what the data shows.

### 4.3 Agentic Configurations

Agentic systems chain many model invocations into a loop of planning, acting, observing and revising. Each link inherits the plausibility mechanism, and the chain adds two aggravating properties. The first is error propagation: a plausible-but-wrong conclusion at an early step becomes an unquestioned premise for every later step, and the final output can be internally coherent while resting on a defect no longer visible in the result. The second is self-assessment: the agent's judgment that a subtask succeeded, that retrieved information suffices or that the task is complete is itself a generated output judged by plausibility. Agents do not know when they are wrong; they produce completion signals that sound like completion. Reflection and self-critique loops help at the margin, but the critic is the same class of model applying the same standard, so agreement between generator and critic is evidence of shared plausibility, not of truth.

Agentic systems accordingly demand the strongest containment posture: bounded scope, checkpoints at consequential actions and human review at any point where an error would become irreversible. The autonomy that makes agents valuable is precisely the removal of the per-step human verification that compensates for the plausibility mechanism.

### 4.4 Other Augmentations

Fine-tuning on domain data shifts which claims are well represented, improving accuracy within the domain without changing the mechanism; the model still fails fluently at the domain's edges. Structured output constraints, such as enforced schemas or mandatory citation fields, discipline form rather than content; a required citation field guarantees a citation-shaped string, not a supporting authority. Model confidence scores measure the system's certainty about text sequences, which correlates imperfectly with factual reliability and should not be presented as an accuracy metric.

## 5. Professional Obligations and Operational Rules

Professional duty frameworks have already absorbed this analysis. The American Bar Association's Formal Opinion 512 provides that lawyers using generative AI must satisfy their duties of competence, confidentiality, communication and candor, and that the degree of independent verification required depends on the tool and the task, with the constant that the tool cannot substitute for the professional's own competent work. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework and its Generative AI Profile likewise treat confabulation as a distinct risk category requiring governance controls rather than a defect awaiting a technical fix. The common principle across these authorities is that fluency shifts none of the verification burden.

The following operational rules translate the analysis into practice:

1. Treat every AI output as a draft produced by a fluent, well-read and unaccountable author with no duty of candor. Verify against authoritative sources before reliance.
2. For any AI workflow, map which segments are verified (tool computation, retrieved source text) and which are generated (query formulation, source selection, synthesis, interpretation, self-assessment). Direct human verification at the generated segments.
3. When output includes citations, verify not only that the source exists but that it supports the stated proposition. Real citations attached to unsupported claims are a signature failure of retrieval-augmented systems.
4. For tool-using systems, confirm that the tool was actually invoked for computational claims, and review the model's interpretation of tool results as generated content.
5. For agentic systems, require bounded scope, logged intermediate steps and human checkpoints before any consequential or irreversible action.
6. In procurement and audit, do not ask whether a system "uses RAG" or "has guardrails." Ask at which points generated content enters the output and what independent check governs each point.
7. Assign named human ownership for every AI-assisted deliverable. A system is only as reliable as its least-verified generative link, and accountability for that link cannot rest with the system.

## 6. Limitations and Cautions

Two cautions apply to the use of this framing. First, it should not be overstated. Well-designed workflows that combine retrieval, tool verification and human review can achieve high reliability; the "sounds good" characterization describes the generative mechanism itself, not the ceiling of what a properly governed system can deliver. Audiences told that augmentation is worthless will discount the entire argument. Second, it should not be understated. Vendor materials frequently present RAG and guardrails as resolving hallucination. The correct message is relocation, not resolution: the augmentation moves the point of failure, and governance must move with it.

## 7. Conclusion

It is accurate, and technically grounded, to characterize AI outputs as "sounding good" rather than being right. The systems are optimized to produce plausible text, correctness is a byproduct rather than a property, and every augmentation now on the market wraps rather than replaces that mechanism. The professional consequence is constant across configurations: verification is a human obligation that attaches wherever generated content enters the work product, and no degree of fluency, grounding or autonomy transfers that obligation to the machine.

**Final Liability rests with the Human.**
